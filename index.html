The Model

How the Minneapolis Fed Built Its Case

The 18% price drop and 7.4% ownership gain come from a structural overlapping generations model calibrated to match actual housing market data across Texas and California. Created by Economic modelers from the Minneapolis fed.

Coven et al (2024) an OLG model. Overlapping generations. Households enter at age 25 with no assets and live for up to 60 years, working for 35 and retiring for 25. Every period they make a choice: rent or own, stay or move, Texas or California. Those choices depend on income, wealth, house prices, property taxes, and the expected cost of carrying housing into aging.

The model has six state variables, two geographies, a full lifecycle of age groups, rental market, down payment constraints set at 20% of purchase price, and a minimum house size requirement. It is calibrated to match homeownership rates, wealth-to-income ratios, migration flows, and income levels in both states. It aims at a 66% ownership rate in Texas and 61% in California, a moving rate of 1.7%, a rent-to-income ratio of 0.30, and a wealth-to-income ratio of 4.4 for the bottom 80% of households. All of those match the data.

For economists: The household utility function is Cobb Douglas over non durable consumption and housing. Households also value bequests and face location-specific amenity benefits from owning versus renting. The key financial friction is the down payment constraint: to buy, you need liquid wealth equal to 20% of the purchase price. That is the binding constraint for most young guys.

Core Model Equations
Selected from Coven et al. (2024)
Working Paper No. 93, Minneapolis Fed
Utility Function (Eq. 11)
U(C, H) = 1/1−σ (Cα H1−α)1−σ + ΞRl
Households value consumption C and housing H. α is calibrated to 0.637. σ = 2 (standard risk aversion). Location amenity Ξ captures CA vs TX preferences.
Down Payment Constraint (Eq. 2)
Yt + (1+r)At = θ · Pi · Ht
To buy, liquid wealth must cover θ = 20% of the home value P·H. When P falls due to higher taxes, the required down payment falls with it. That is the capitalization channel in one equation.
User Cost / Rent-Price Link (Eq. 13)
Ri = φi · Pi = (τi + r + δ) · Pi
Rent equals user cost times price. When τ (property tax rate) rises, the user cost rises, so buyers discount P downward. This is the price-capitalization mechanism that drives the 18% CA result.

Bequest motive Ψ is a key driver of elderly lock-in: older households accumulate housing partly to pass it on, which keeps them in large homes longer than their own needs require.

Analysis

Evidence Takeaway

Horwich (2024) explains that higher property taxes get capitalized into lower home prices, which means buyers pay less upfront because they expect to pay more over time. It shows why the same tax can feel punishing to one group and helpful to another. Existing homeowners feel the bill right away, while younger buyers may benefit from a lower purchase price and a weaker entry barrier.

Walczak (2024) argues that property taxes do less economic harm than many other taxes and that is the core reason economists keep defending them. Income taxes can discourage work and investment. Sales taxes can discourage spending. Property taxes are different because they are tied to immovable assets like land and buildings, so they are harder to avoid and less likely to distort labor or production decisions in the same way. His argument is that they are a better tax base than many any of the alternatives.

"For older homeowners, property taxes affect incentives to sell or stay put. Low property taxes make it financially advantageous to sit tight, even if the nest has long been empty—a powerful “lock-in” effect. Higher property taxes, by contrast, raise the motivation to downsize, which puts more homes on the market for young families who will make use of those empty bedrooms"

Jeff Horwich, Minneapolis Fed (2024)

Horwich also explains that higher property taxes can push older adults to downsize and can shift homeownership toward younger families, which means the tax does not just raise revenue. It changes who stays and who can get into the housing market in the first place.

Walczak argues against assessment limitations like Proposition 13, explaining how such measures create perverse incentives that lock existing homeowners in place, unfairly shift the tax burden to younger and lower income buyers, and ultimately worsen the very housing affordability crisis they were supposed to address. So even though the tax is visible and politically toxic, it looks better than many alternatives when you compare efficiency, stability, and housing market effects.
